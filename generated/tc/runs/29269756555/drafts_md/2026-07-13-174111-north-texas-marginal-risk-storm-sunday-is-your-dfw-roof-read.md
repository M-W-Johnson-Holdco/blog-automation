## What does a Marginal Risk storm actually mean for a Dallas-Fort Worth roof?

A Marginal Risk means isolated severe storms are possible but not widespread — yet the hail, gusty winds, and heavy downpours forecast for Sunday are enough to damage vulnerable roofs (Source: [cbsnews.com](https://www.cbsnews.com/texas/video/tracking-scattered-storm-chances-for-sunday/), July 2026). Wind gusts pry at shingle edges that have lost their adhesive seal over years of Texas heat, while hail bruises the mat and knocks off the protective granule layer. Heavy rain then exploits every opening — a lifted shingle, a cracked pipe boot, a tired valley. In Denton County and northern Collin County, where many roofs are 15 to 25 years into their service life, a "minor" storm is often the one that turns a hidden weak spot into an active roof leak. The takeaway: treat every storm as a stress test and document your roof's baseline condition now.

## Why are aging DFW suburban roofs more vulnerable to routine storms?

Aging roofs fail during ordinary storms because the materials have already lost the flexibility and adhesion they need to resist wind and hail. Much of the Dallas-Fort Worth housing stock — the 1980s through early-2000s tract builds filling Plano, Frisco, and McKinney — sits on original or first-replacement asphalt shingle roofs now nearing the end of their expected lifespan. Decades of intense UV exposure make shingles brittle, so a gusty-wind event that a new roof would ignore can crease or tear older ones. Heavy rain like Sunday's forecast finds the path of least resistance through deteriorated flashing and dried-out pipe boots. If your roof predates 2010, the practical step is a documented condition assessment before storm damage accumulates unnoticed under the surface.

### How do I tell storm damage from normal roof wear?

Storm damage typically appears as fresh, clustered marks — hail bruises, creased shingles, or torn tabs — while normal wear shows up as uniform aging across the whole roof. Damage tied to a specific storm date is easier to attribute than gradual, roof-wide aging. A licensed contractor can distinguish the two in a written report, which matters when an insurer reviews your claim. Getting a documented baseline before the storm removes most of the guesswork.
